1. What is punk?

The sound

The basic punk sound is vocals, electric guitar, electric bass, and drums playing a song with a simple chord structure, generous distortion, and an uptempo beat.

You don't have to be particularly good at your instrument.
Sometimes it helps not to be.

The attitude

Aggressive, emotional, ferocious, youthful.

The ethos

Do-it-yourself (DIY). Write your own songs, record in your own basement, release your own albums, organize your own concerts. Don't lose control of your artistic product.

Fugazi was particularly well respected for this - for years they released albums on their own label and arranged their own shows.

The politics

Generally left-wing. Identification with the poor and working class. Distrust of the government and of the wealthy. Some revolutionary rhetoric.

Some far-right, often unsavory political movements have also adopted punk.
Other politically-oriented punk fans hate them probably worst of all.

Different people think different aspects of punk are the most important, and so it ends up meaning different things from person to person.

2. Sped up rock 'n' roll

The root influence of the punk sound is just older-style rock 'n' roll, played faster and louder and with more rawness.

Sometimes this influence can be heard explicitly in the form of a cover.

Soul Kitchen

The Doors (1967) covered by X (1980). The album with X's rendition was produced by Ray Manzarek, the Doors' keyboardist.

3. Trajectories

Led by the Sex Pistols and the Ramones, punk exploded into popular music in the late 1970s.

Many bands quickly took punk and ran in new directions.

4. Alternative

Punk grew steadily in the 1980s, but its popularity really took off in the early 1990s.

The sound of punk permanently entered the mainstream, somewhat ironically in a form labeled "alternative."

Post-punk

In the 1980s American "post-punk" bands kept the distorted electric guitar sound but slowed things down, got more melodious, wrote more elaborate song structures, and emphasized artistry over attitude and politics.

This style of music is probably my personal favorite.

Nirvana

In the late 1980s and early 1990s, this genre burst into widespread prominence, spearheaded by Nirvana and the Seattle scene from which they came.

During this time many similar bands were also signed out of more underground scenes by major labels, receiving national exposure.

These bands drew from a variety of influences of which punk was just one. (Folk, art rock, metal, country, hopefully a little made-up brand new shit too...)

But you can continue to hear the sounds of old 70s-era punk in music that is considered very mainstream today.

5. The spirit of punk

So far we've emphasized the sonic qualities of what people think of as punk. But for some people, "punk" has more to do a way of thinking and an artistic approach than what the actual music sounds like.

To them, the most important thing about "punk" isn't how it sounds, but the idea of pushing artistic boundaries, spreading messages of social justice, and DIY.

"...But everyone who [did] started a band"

The Velvet Underground is considered a major forerunner of punk, even though they don't sound much like the late 1970s punk bands.

They had a big influence on many musicians that took the punk sound and went in more melodically and lyrically thoughtful directions.

Quieter stuff

A lot of bands who liked punk music retained the ethos and the emotion but adopted a lighter, "poppier" sound.

Beat Happening doesn't sound like punk. But they and their label K Records have been active participants and supporters of punk bands, and have remained fiercely independent and DIY.

Riot grrl

In the early 1990s many feminist-minded women took to punk to empower themselves and give voice to women's issues and perspectives.

Punk is sadly not free of sexism; hopefully we can get to the point where "women's bands" are just "bands." I think things are getting better.

Noise

Many people feel that you aren't really "punk" if you aren't exploring new sounds and pushing the boundaries of music.

Some people consider this unlistenable.
I really like Lightning Bolt. These bands are not for everyone though.

The mainstream

The other side: some people consider mainstream pop-punk songs to be so far removed from the ideas of punk as not to merit the label.

I like plenty of pop music, punk-influenced and not. I don't care to debate whether something is "punk" or not. But some people do.

You could argue that "punk" is such a broadly defined, disagreed-upon term as to have little meaning beyond something generally positive about music.

I wouldn't wholly disagree. But the label has come to stand for a lot of the kinds of bands and songs that I like, which is why I think it's worth talking about.

The end

These five lessons only covered a small fraction of punk music in a really simplified way. I hope they've inspired you to go listen to and learn about the rest!
